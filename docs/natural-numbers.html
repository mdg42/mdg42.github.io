Section 1.1 The Natural Numbers and Sets

When we first learn to count, we begin in the natural way of doing so, starting with \(1\text{,}\) then \(2\text{,}\) and so forth. These numbers are referred to as the Natural numbers or counting numbers. In some cases, \(0\) is included in the Natural numbers, and other times its inclusion is called the Whole numbers. In mathematics, we refer to this collection of things as a set. The items within the set are called elements or members. For example, we could say that \(5\) is an element of the set of Natural numbers.

To write small sets, we use curly braces. For example, we could write a set of Natural numbers as \(\{1, 2, 5, 7\}\text{.}\) While this works well for small sets, clearly we cannot write all the Natural numbers this way. Additionally, writing "the Natural numbers" would prove difficult in many contexts where they are used, so we use the symbol \(\mathbb{N}\) to represent the set of Natural numbers.
